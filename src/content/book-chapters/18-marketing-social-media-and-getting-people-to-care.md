I thought launching MemeScanr into the App Store meant I had distributed it. What I had actually done was place it on a shelf in a warehouse with four hundred other photo cleaner apps and hope that strangers would somehow walk down the right aisle.

For two weeks after launch, I did almost no marketing. I told myself the product was good enough that people would find it. I checked the download numbers every morning. They were not zero, but they were close. Three downloads. Seven downloads. Four downloads. The numbers felt like a verdict.

Then, on a Thursday night, I posted a fifteen-second TikTok. I showed MemeScanr's scan results screen with the line "your phone needs therapy" and a caption that said *"I built this app because my camera roll was making me feel like a bad person."* I almost didn't post it. It felt too personal. It felt too small.

That TikTok got 47,000 views. Not viral by TikTok standards. But for an app with eleven total downloads, it was a different universe. I got 200 downloads in two days. I got DMs from people asking if this was real. I got my first App Store reviews from strangers.

The product hadn't changed. The App Store listing hadn't changed. The only thing that changed was that I had told one specific group of people, in their language, on their platform, why this thing might matter to them.

That's the night I understood: **good products do not market themselves. Builders have to create paths to discovery.** Everything in this chapter comes from that realization.

I want to say this directly, because it's one of the biggest blocks for solo builders: **marketing is not betrayal.** If your product genuinely helps people, then helping the right people discover it is part of the job, not an annoying extra. Visibility is not vanity when it serves value. The best marketing still sounds human. Do not let fear of "cringe" keep you invisible.

### The App Store Is Not a Distribution Strategy

This is the most important idea in this chapter, so I want to say it plainly: **the App Store is not a distribution strategy. The App Store is a *result* of a distribution strategy.**

I used to think "I'll put the app in the store and people will find it." That's not how it works. People find apps because of other things they saw first:

- A TikTok they scrolled past
- A Reddit thread they searched for
- A friend who DM'd them the app
- An article they read on a blog
- A search result on Google
- An In-App Event shown in their App Store
- A recommendation from an AI chatbot

The App Store listing converts the visit into an install. But the visit comes from somewhere else. And "somewhere else" is a **portfolio of channels**, not a single source.

My MemeScanr distribution portfolio looks roughly like this:

- **memescanr.com** (SEO-optimized, handles long-tail searches about "best photo cleaner app for iPhone")
- **afialabs.net** (the parent brand, builds trust for future products)
- **TikTok** (short videos about specific features, voice-driven, low production value)
- **Reddit** (occasional posts in relevant subreddits, honest and useful, not promotional)
- **App Store optimization** (keyword-optimized metadata, screenshots, In-App Events)
- **Word of mouth** (users sharing Wrapped cards, screenshots, App Store reviews)
- **Editorial placement** (occasional features in Apple's editorial lists, mostly luck but nudged by consistent In-App Events)

No single channel is "the" channel. Each one contributes a small stream of users. Together they add up. If TikTok's algorithm changes, I still have SEO. If SEO dies, I still have In-App Events. The portfolio is the strategy.

**Build the portfolio slowly, one channel at a time.** Pick one, get good at it, then add another. The worst thing you can do is try to show up on every platform at once. Two years from now you'll have a portfolio. Today you'll have a list of channels you're about to start.

### The Post That Almost Didn't Happen

I want to go back to that TikTok, because it taught me something I didn't expect.

I had been sitting on MemeScanr for two weeks, telling myself I'd start marketing "when the app is ready." The app was already in the App Store. It was ready. What I really meant was "when I feel ready," which is a different thing entirely and has no fixed arrival date.

The TikTok I posted that Thursday was not planned. I didn't script it. I didn't use good lighting. I didn't have a content strategy. I opened the app, screen-recorded the scan results, added a caption that sounded like something I'd text a friend, and posted it while sitting on my couch.

The reason it worked was not production value. It was specificity. I didn't say "download my photo cleanup app." I said "my camera roll was making me feel like a bad person." That sentence resonated because it was a feeling people recognized but had never heard named. The app was the solution. The feeling was the hook.

**The lesson I took: marketing as a solo builder is not about looking professional. It's about being specific enough that the right person thinks "wait, that's me."**

Every piece of marketing I've made since then follows the same pattern. Lead with a feeling the target user recognizes. Show the product as the answer to that feeling. Use the language they'd use, not the language a marketer would use.

### What Counts as Marketing

If you hate marketing, make it easier on yourself. Here's what still counts:

- Post a screenshot of a feature you just shipped
- Post a lesson you learned while building
- Post a mistake and how you fixed it
- Post a before-and-after improvement
- Post a user's reaction that surprised you

All of that is marketing. It works better than glossy ads for a solo builder, because it's honest and specific and it shows the human behind the product.

You don't need to go viral. You need to be visible long enough to be memorable. **Virality is luck. Memorability is discipline.**

### Build in Public (Without Performing)

Building in public is valuable, but it has a failure mode worth naming.

**The healthy version** is: you share what you're working on as you work on it. The posts are honest, specific, and useful to other builders. You share the wins and the losses. Other builders find you, follow you, and some of them become early users. This is a slow, compounding distribution channel.

**The failure mode** is: you start treating it as performance. You post every minor update as if it were big news. You manufacture drama. You share metrics without context. You post more than you build. Builders recognize the pattern and tune out.

The difference is mostly about **who you're writing for.**

Healthy: *"I'm writing this because it might help another builder who's two steps behind me."*

Failure: *"I'm writing this because I want attention."*

My rule: **only post things you'd still be glad you posted in six months, even if nobody engaged with them.** Performative posts are only worth writing if they work. Honest posts are worth writing whether they work or not.

### Your Website Is Infrastructure, Not a Nice-to-Have

Here's a thing I didn't understand at first: **your App Store page is a product page, not a brand page.** It's optimized for conversion in one specific moment. It's not designed to build a relationship over time. For that, you need a website.

memescanr.com and afialabs.net exist because the App Store is not enough. The App Store shows you MemeScanr when a user is browsing for cleanup apps. Outside that moment, it does nothing for you. It doesn't send emails. It doesn't run blog posts. It doesn't show up in Google searches for questions your target user has.

A website does all of those things.

Every serious solo builder should have a website for their app, even if it's simple. What matters is that it exists, is on a domain you own, and contains: the founder story, the core pitch with screenshots, a privacy policy, a way to contact you, and ideally a newsletter signup. memescanr.com started at about 6 pages. It's now 14. It cost me almost nothing to set up. It sends me organic traffic every single day, which is more than the App Store does when my app isn't featured.

### Why Your Website Matters for AI-Driven Discovery

There's a specific reason the website is more important in 2026 than it was in 2020: **AI chatbots are becoming a real part of how users discover apps.**

When a user asks ChatGPT, Claude, or Perplexity "what's the best photo cleaner app for iPhone," those tools don't search the App Store. They search the web. If your app has no website presence, you don't exist to these tools. If you have a good website with clear content about what your app does and who it's for, you get recommended.

This is free distribution. Nobody is charging you for it. But you have to show up in the web index for it to work. memescanr.com is optimized for this: clear content on every page, a robots.txt that allows AI crawlers, JSON-LD schema tags on the FAQ pages. None of it was difficult to set up. The benefit is showing up in AI responses when users ask about photo cleanup apps.

If you're building in 2026 and you don't have a website, you're leaving free distribution on the table. The 20 hours it takes to build a simple site will pay back for years.

### A Simple Posting Rhythm

If you're wondering what to actually post and when:

**Monday — Process.** Something about the work. A bug you fixed, a design decision, a moment you changed your mind.

**Wednesday — Product.** A specific feature, screenshot, or use case. Show the thing.

**Friday — Reflection.** What you learned this week. Short, honest, specific.

Three posts a week. Not overwhelming. Not silent. Consistent enough that people start recognizing your name.

### Case Study — The Screenshot That Changed the Conversion Rate

I want to close with a specific marketing story, because it's the clearest example I have of how small, specific marketing decisions can have outsized impact.

MemeScanr's original App Store screenshots were clean and informative. They showed the scan results screen, the duplicate grid, the settings page. They were accurate. They were also boring. They looked like every other photo cleanup app's screenshots.

For about three months, my conversion rate from App Store page visit to download was hovering around 18%. Not terrible. Not good. I knew the screenshots were the problem, but I kept telling myself "the product is what matters, people will download it when they see the features."

Then I changed the first screenshot. Instead of showing the scan results screen with the headline "Smart Scan," I showed the same screen with the headline "your phone needs therapy." Same feature. Same UI. Different words.

Conversion rate went from 18% to 31% in ten days.

The product hadn't changed. The price hadn't changed. The app was identical. I changed five words on one screenshot, and the download rate nearly doubled.

**That's what marketing actually is for a solo builder.** Not a budget. Not a strategy deck. Not a content calendar. It's finding the five words that make the right person stop scrolling and think *"wait, that's me."*

### > Think Before You Move On

What's the one social platform where your target user already spends time? Not where you wish they spent time. Where they actually hang out. Commit to that platform for one month. Post three times a week. See what happens.
