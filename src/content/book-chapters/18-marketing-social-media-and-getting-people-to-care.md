---A lot of builders secretly want the product to speak for itself.

That sounds nice. It also usually is not enough.

In the real world, people need to know your app exists before they can love it. They need to hear about it more than once. They need to understand why it matters to them. They need a reason to care.

That is where marketing comes in. And marketing is not just ads.

It is storytelling. It is positioning. It is repetition. It is trust-building. It is showing the right people the right message enough times that it sticks.

### Marketing Is Not Betrayal

Some people treat marketing like it somehow makes the work less pure. But if your app actually helps people, telling people about it is not annoying. It is part of the service.

Good marketing is clarity. Good marketing is empathy. Good marketing is translation. It helps the right person understand that what you built might matter to them.

I want to say this clearly because it's one of the biggest blocks for solo builders: **visibility is not vanity when it serves value.**

If your app can genuinely help someone, then helping the right people discover it is part of the responsibility, not an annoying extra. Marketing done well is not manipulation. It is alignment. It helps the right message meet the right person at the right time.

The best marketing still sounds human. Your app deserves visibility. Do not let fear of "cringe" keep you invisible.

### Social Media Gives Indie Builders the Best Shot They've Ever Had

Social media matters because it gives you a place to show what you are building, talk about the problem your product solves, attract early adopters, build curiosity before launch, share updates after launch, create brand personality, and turn users into advocates.

If you hate marketing, make it easier on yourself. Here's what still counts as marketing:

- Post screenshots.
- Post progress videos.
- Post founder thoughts.
- Post lessons learned.
- Post mistakes and fixes.
- Post feature reveals.
- Post before-and-after improvements.
- Post user wins.

All of that is marketing. And it works better than glossy ads for a solo indie builder, because it's honest and specific and it shows the human behind the product.

This matters even more now because AI is making it easier than ever for more people to build products. That means more competition. When more people can build, attention becomes even more valuable.

So yes, build a good product. But also build visibility. Because the best app in the world still struggles if nobody knows it exists.

### Distribution Is a Portfolio

One thing I didn't understand when I launched MemeScanr: **the App Store is not a distribution strategy. The App Store is a *result* of a distribution strategy.**

Here's what I mean. I used to think "I'll put the app in the store and people will find it." That's not how it works. People find apps because of other things they saw first:

- A TikTok they scrolled past
- A Reddit thread they searched for
- A friend who DM'd them the app
- An article they read on a blog
- A search result on Google
- An In-App Event shown in their App Store
- A recommendation from the editorial team

The App Store listing converts the visit into an install. But the visit comes from somewhere else. And "somewhere else" is a **portfolio of channels**, not a single source.

My MemeScanr distribution portfolio looks roughly like this:

- **memescanr.com** (SEO-optimized, handles long-tail searches about "best photo cleaner app for iPhone")
- **afialabs.com** (the parent brand, builds trust for future products)
- **TikTok** (short videos about specific features, voice-driven, low production value)
- **Reddit** (occasional posts in relevant subreddits, honest and useful, not promotional)
- **App Store optimization** (keyword-optimized metadata, screenshots, In-App Events)
- **Word of mouth** (users sharing Wrapped cards, screenshots, App Store reviews)
- **Editorial placement** (occasional features in Apple's editorial lists, which are mostly luck but can be nudged by consistently using In-App Events)

No single channel is "the" channel. Each one contributes a small stream of users. Together they add up. The portfolio is resilient in a way that any single channel would not be. If TikTok's algorithm changes, I still have SEO. If SEO dies, I still have In-App Events. The portfolio is the strategy.

**Build the portfolio slowly, one channel at a time, and keep the channels you can maintain without burning out.** The worst thing you can do is try to show up on every platform at once. Pick one, get good at it, then add another. Two years from now you'll have a portfolio. One year from now you'll have your first real channel. Today you'll have a list of channels you're about to start.

### Build in Public (Without Performing)

Building in public is a real thing, and it's real valuable, and it also has a failure mode that's worth naming.

**The healthy version of building in public** is: you share what you're working on as you work on it. The posts are honest, specific, and useful to other builders. You share the wins and the losses. You explain decisions. You show the product evolving. Other builders find you, follow you, and some of them become early users or supporters. This is a slow, compounding distribution channel, and it's one of the best things a solo builder can do for their brand.

**The failure mode** is: you start treating building in public as performance instead of sharing. You post every minor update as if it were big news. You manufacture drama. You share metrics without context. You celebrate milestones that aren't actually milestones. You post more than you build. Other builders start to recognize the pattern and tune out.

The difference between healthy and failure-mode is subtle. It's mostly about **who you're writing for.**

Healthy mode is: *"I'm writing this post because I think it would help another builder who's two steps behind me."*

Failure mode is: *"I'm writing this post because I want attention."*

Both can look similar on the outside. The internal intention is what determines whether the posts land or flop over time. Readers are smart. They can feel the difference, even when they can't articulate it.

My rule for building in public: **only post things that you would still be glad you posted in six months, even if nobody engaged with them.** This filter eliminates most performative posts, because performative posts are only worth writing if they work. Honest posts are worth writing whether they work or not.

MemeScanr's build-in-public story is messy. I share the wins. I share the losses. I share the Apple rejection stories. I share the bugs. I share the moments I wanted to quit. I don't share metrics I don't understand. I don't pretend to be further along than I am. That's it. That's the whole strategy.

### The Website Is Where Your Brand Lives Between App Updates

Here's a thing I didn't understand at first: **your App Store page is a product page, not a brand page.** It's optimized for conversion in a specific moment (the decision to download). It's not designed to build a brand over time. For that, you need a website.

memescanr.com and afialabs.com exist because the App Store is not enough. The App Store shows you MemeScanr at a specific moment: when a user is browsing for cleanup apps. Outside that moment, the App Store doesn't do any work for you. The App Store doesn't send emails. The App Store doesn't run blog posts. The App Store doesn't show up in Google searches for questions your target user has.

A website does all of those things. And more importantly, a website lets you build a relationship with users across time, not just in one moment.

My rule: **every serious solo builder should have a website for their app, even if the website is simple.** Simple is fine. Ugly is fine. Low-traffic is fine. What matters is that the website exists, is on a domain you own, and contains:

1. The founder story (one page)
2. The core pitch for the app (one page, with screenshots)
3. A few feature pages that go deeper on the things that matter (optional, but great for SEO)
4. A privacy policy and terms (required for App Store compliance anyway)
5. A way to contact you (email or form)
6. A newsletter signup (optional, but valuable long-term)

memescanr.com is about 14 pages now: the home page, 5 feature pages, 8 FAQ pages, plus legal. It was free to set up (hosted on a static site generator, pushed to a free hosting provider). It took me about 20 hours spread over a few weeks. It sends me organic traffic every single day, which is more than the App Store does when my app isn't featured.

**The website is not a nice-to-have. It's infrastructure.** Treat it that way.

### Why a Website Matters for AI-Driven Discovery

There's a specific reason the website is more important in 2026 than it was in 2020: **AI chatbots are becoming a meaningful part of how users discover apps.**

When a user asks ChatGPT, Claude, or Perplexity "what's the best photo cleaner app for iPhone," those tools don't search the App Store. They search the web. They read blog posts, comparison articles, and product pages on websites. If your app has no website presence, you don't exist to these tools. If you have a good website with clear content about what your app does and who it's for, you get recommended.

This is free distribution. Nobody is charging you for it. But you have to show up in the web index for it to work. That means having a real website, not just an App Store listing.

memescanr.com is optimized for this. Every page has clear content that describes what MemeScanr does, who it's for, and how it compares to alternatives. There's a robots.txt that explicitly allows AI crawlers (GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, etc.). There are JSON-LD schema.org tags on the FAQ pages so AI tools can parse them correctly. None of this was difficult to set up. The benefit is showing up in AI responses when users ask about photo cleanup apps.

If you're building in 2026 and you don't have a website yet, you're leaving free distribution on the table. The 20 hours it takes to build a simple website will pay back in AI-driven traffic for years. Do it.

### What to Post and When

If you're wondering what to actually post and when, here's a simple weekly rhythm:

**Monday — Process.** Something about the work. A bug you fixed, a design decision, a moment you changed your mind.

**Wednesday — Product.** A specific feature, screenshot, or use case. Show the thing.

**Friday — Reflection.** What you learned this week. Short, honest, specific.

That's three posts a week, one per platform. Not overwhelming. Not silent. Consistent enough that people start recognizing your name.

You don't need to go viral. You need to be visible long enough to be rememberable. Virality is luck. Memorability is discipline.

### Case Study — The Builder Who Posted Before She Felt Ready

A builder keeps waiting for her app to feel "real enough" before talking about it. Weeks go by. Then months. She is improving the product, but nobody knows it exists.

Eventually she starts sharing simple screenshots, small lessons, and tiny progress updates. To her surprise, people respond. Not because the posts are perfect, but because they are honest. Someone says, "I need this." Another person shares a use case she had not considered. A third joins the waitlist.

Nothing viral happens. But something better does. Momentum becomes social. And that changes the emotional experience of building. The app is no longer only living in her head. It is beginning to live in the world.

### > Think Before You Move On

What's the one social platform where your target user already spends time? Not where you wish they spent time. Where they actually hang out. Commit to that platform for one month. Post three times a week. See what happens.
